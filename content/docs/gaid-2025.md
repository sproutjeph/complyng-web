## Part I — Application and Commencement

GAID 2025 is the implementation directive issued by the Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC) under section 61 of the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023. It commenced on 19 September 2025 and applies to all Data Controllers and Data Processors handling personal data of Nigerian residents, whether the controller is established in Nigeria or not. GAID operationalises the Act by specifying forms, procedures, deadlines, fees, and sectoral expectations.

---

## Part II — Data Controllers and Processors of Major Importance (DCMI / DPMI)

An organisation is classified as a Data Controller of Major Importance (DCMI) or Data Processor of Major Importance (DPMI) where it meets any of the following criteria:

1. Processes personal data of 500 or more Nigerian data subjects annually;
2. Processes sensitive personal data (health, biometric, genetic, political, religious, sexual, financial-credential) of any volume;
3. Operates in a designated sector: financial services, telecommunications, health, digital platforms, insurance, education;
4. Cross-border transfers personal data of Nigerian residents;
5. Conducts automated decision-making with legal or similarly significant effect.

Classification triggers the registration and filing obligations in Parts III and V.

## Part III — Registration of DCMIs and DPMIs

**Article 9 — Registration window.** DCMIs and DPMIs must register with NDPC via the NDPC Compliance Portal by submitting GAID Form A within six (6) months of first meeting the threshold or of commencement of operations in Nigeria, whichever is later.

**Article 10 — Information required.** Form A captures legal entity details, nature of processing, categories of personal data, volumes, retention periods, sub-processors, and Data Protection Officer contact details.

**Article 11 — DPO designation.** The DPO must hold a data-protection certification recognised by NDPC (e.g. CIPM, CIPP/E, or NDPC-licensed DPCO certification). Designation must be notified via the Compliance Portal within thirty (30) days and reaffirmed at each annual filing.

**Article 12 — Amendment of registration.** Material changes to the information in Form A must be notified to NDPC within thirty (30) days.

---

## Part IV — Licensing of Data Protection Compliance Organisations (DPCOs)

**Article 14.** Only NDPC-licensed DPCOs may conduct the annual compliance audit required under Part V. The NDPC maintains a public register of licensed DPCOs. DPCO licences are renewed annually and subject to inspection powers.

**Article 15 — Conflicts.** A DPCO may not audit an organisation in which it has a financial interest, nor one it has advised on the same compliance programme in the reporting year.

---

## Part V — Annual Compliance Returns

**Article 18 — Form C filing.** Every DCMI/DPMI must file the Annual Compliance Return (GAID Form C) by 31 March of each year, covering the preceding calendar year. Form C must be attested by an NDPC-licensed DPCO.

**Article 19 — Contents of Form C.**
- Record of Processing Activities (RoPA) summary;
- Data Protection Impact Assessment register;
- Breach register and incident summaries;
- Data subject rights request metrics;
- Cross-border transfer register;
- Processor due-diligence attestations;
- Training attendance summary;
- Audit findings and remediation status.

**Article 20 — Retention.** Audit logs supporting Form C (access, modification, deletion logs relating to personal data) must be retained for a minimum of seven (7) years.

---

## Part VI — Cross-Border Transfers

**Article 22 — Lawful basis.** Transfers of personal data outside Nigeria require one of: (a) an NDPC adequacy decision (GAID Schedule 3 whitelist); (b) Standard Contractual Clauses approved by NDPC; (c) Binding Corporate Rules approved by NDPC; or (d) an explicit derogation under NDPA s.43.

**Article 23 — Register.** The transfer register must be maintained and updated within thirty (30) days of any new transfer, capturing recipient, country, volume, adequacy basis, and safeguards.

---

## Part VII — Processor Engagements

**Article 26 — Data Processing Agreements.** Every engagement of a processor or sub-processor must be governed by a written DPA containing, at minimum:

1. Subject matter, duration, nature, and purpose of processing;
2. Categories of personal data and data subjects;
3. Obligations and rights of the controller;
4. Security-of-processing measures;
5. Sub-processor authorisation and notification procedure;
6. Data-subject rights support clause;
7. Breach notification timeline (≤ 24 hours processor-to-controller);
8. Audit rights;
9. Return-or-destroy clause at termination.

**Article 27 — Due diligence.** The controller must document the processor's technical and organisational measures and retain this record as part of the Form C filing.

---

## Part VIII — Enforcement and Breach Notification

**Article 29 — Breach notification.** Notifications to NDPC must use the Schedule 2 template and be filed within seventy-two (72) hours of the controller becoming aware of the breach. Where risk to data subjects is high, the controller must also notify affected individuals without undue delay.

**Article 30 — Penalty formula.** Administrative sanctions are the greater of (a) a fixed schedule amount or (b) two per cent (2%) of the organisation's annual gross revenue, subject to Part VIII caps. Non-filing, false filing, or failure to appoint a DPO each constitutes a separate contravention.

---

## Part IX — Sensitive Populations

**Article 32 — Staff training.** Role-appropriate data-protection training must be delivered to all staff handling personal data at least once per calendar year. Attendance records and training materials must be retained for NDPC inspection.

**Article 34 — Children.** Where an online service is likely to be accessed by children under eighteen (18), the controller must operate a verifiable parental-consent mechanism and document the method of verification. Age-assurance measures are expected proportional to risk.

---

## Part X — Automated Decision-Making

**Article 38.** Where personal data is subjected to solely-automated decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects, the controller must:

1. Disclose the existence of the automated decision in the privacy notice;
2. Provide meaningful information about the logic involved;
3. Offer data subjects the right to request human review;
4. Log each automated decision with inputs, outputs, and any human-review outcome;
5. Test the model for bias annually and document the results.
